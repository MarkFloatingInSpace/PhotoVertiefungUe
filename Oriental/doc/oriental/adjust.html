Non-linear, robust least-squares adjustment

oriental.adjust provides

  • a Python-interface to ceres, see the ceres manual.

  • predefined observation types, with definitions following ORIENT

  • additional functionality to estimate a posteriori variances, etc.

For an explanation on the dependencies among the various options for solving, see Solver.Options.

class PhotoDistortion¶

Bases: Boost.Python.enum

Photo distortion coefficients

We substitute the principal point \((x_0,y_0)\) by the following equations:

\[\begin{split}x_0 &:= x_0 + \sum_{i}(a_i \cdot dx_0(x_n,y_n) )\\ y_0 &:= y_0 + \sum_{i}(a_i \cdot dy_0(x_n,y_n) )\\\end{split}\]

Using the distortion coefficients \(a_i\) and the following definitions:

\[\begin{split}k &= \left\{ \begin{array}{lr} 0 & \text{ for} \text{ adpReferencePoint} ==& \text{origin}\\ 1 & \text{ for} \text{ adpReferencePoint} ==& \text{principalPoint} \end{array} \right.\\ \rho_0 &: \text{Normalization radius (also the radius of the zero-value of radially symmetrical distortion) }\\ x_n &:= (x_{image} - k \cdot x_0) / \rho_0\\ y_n &:= (y_{image} - k \cdot y_0) / \rho_0\\ r^2 &:= x_n^2 + y_n^2\\\end{split}\]

\(x_n\), \(y_n\) are normalized, reduced image coordinates now.

Value

Description

affinitySkewnessOfAxes
\(dx_0=0\)
\(dy_0=x_n\)
Torlegard, Bauer/Mueller, Brown affinity - skewness of axes (ORIENT: 1)

affinityScaleOfYAxis
\(dx_0=0\)
\(dy_0=y_n\)
Torlegard, Bauer/Mueller, Brown affinity - scaling of y-axis (ORIENT: 2)

optPolynomRadial3
\(dx_0=x_n \cdot (r^2-1)\)
\(dy_0=y_n \cdot (r^2-1)\)
Torlegard, opt. polynomial radial distortion; 3.degree (ORIENT: 3)

optPolynomRadial5
\(dx_0=x_n \cdot (r^4-1)\)
\(dy_0=y_n \cdot (r^4-1)\)
Torlegard, opt. polynomial radial distortion; 5.degree (ORIENT: 4)

optPolynomTangential1
\(dx_0=r^2+2 \cdot x_n^2\)
\(dy_0=2 \cdot x_n \cdot y_n\)
Torlegard, opt. polynomial tangential(asymmetric) dist. (ORIENT: 5)

optPolynomTangential2
\(dx_0=2 \cdot x_n \cdot y_n\)
\(dy_0=r^2 + 2 \cdot y_n^2\)
Torlegard, opt. polynomial tangential(asymmetric) dist (ORIENT: 6)

optPolynomRadial7
\(dx_0=x_n \cdot (r^6-1)\)
\(dy_0=y_n \cdot (r^6-1)\)
Torlegard, opt. polynomial radial distortion; 7.degree (ORIENT: 37)

optPolynomRadial9
\(dx_0=x_n \cdot (r^8-1)\)
\(dy_0=y_n \cdot (r^8-1)\)
Torlegard, opt. polynomial radial distortion; 9.degree (ORIENT: 38)

optPolynomRadial11
\(dx_0=x_n \cdot (r^{10}-1)\)
\(dy_0=y_n \cdot (r^{10}-1)\)
Torlegard, opt. polynomial radial distortion; 11.degree (ORIENT: 39)

The following plot illustrates the effects of the most common distortion functions. The black grid denotes the image plane, while the red, distorted grid shows the locations where the principal point is shifted to. The parameter values have been selected such that they are positive, and such that the shift lengths are the same in the image corners. The circle defined by \(\rho_0\) is shown in blue.

\(\rho_0\) serves two purposes:

  • enhance numerical stability

  • make lens distortion parameters of cameras with different sensor resolutions better comparable.

As can be seen, the effect of radial distortion compensation functions is zero at the principal point and at the circle defined by \(\rho_0\).
